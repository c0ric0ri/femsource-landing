The problem

The research exists.
Your doctor just hasn't read it yet.

Women were excluded from clinical trials until 1993. Today, 58% of medical textbooks worldwide contain no reference to menopause at all. Physicians aren't failing women intentionally — but they're working from a system that was never designed with women in mind, and guidelines that take 17 years to catch up with new evidence.

That gap affects everything: from managing perimenopause symptoms and understanding PCOS, to the prevention of conditions that disproportionately affect women like Alzheimer's, osteoporosis, cardiovascular disease, and breast cancer.

Endometriosis Surgery FemSource

"Is excision surgery actually better than ablation for endometriosis pain?"

The evidence on excision versus ablation is less clear-cut than you'd expect, and the recurrence data is what most women aren't told

Bias & funding

Low-moderate risk

Institutional & academic funding · No pharma sponsorship identified

Confidence

Moderate

Mixed results depending on disease stage · Poor outcome standardisation limits comparison

Peer review status

Peer reviewed · Published in J Minim Invasive Gynecol, Reprod Fertil, Fertil Steril

Sponsors

Abbott et al.: University of New South Wales, Sydney (no pharma) · Pickett et al.: UC San Diego academic centre (no pharma) · Burks et al.: academic institutional funding (no pharma sponsorship identified)

Study population

Systematic review: 38 studies · Individual RCTs: n=20–300 · Cohort studies: n up to several thousand

Demography

Women of reproductive age with confirmed endometriosis · North American & European centres

Key limitation

Outcome reporting is poorly standardised. Pain scales, follow-up periods, and definitions of recurrence vary across studies.

Date published

2021 systematic review · 2023 current care review · Supporting studies 2004–2026

Why this matters to you

If you've been told excision is always the better option, the evidence is more nuanced than that. The type and location of your lesions matter — for some presentations the depth of removal makes a real difference, for others the research shows no meaningful advantage over ablation. What almost never comes up before surgery is that roughly one in five women needs a repeat procedure within two years, regardless of which technique was used. That's not a reason to avoid surgery. It's a reason to ask better questions before you agree to it.

What the research found

  • For ovarian endometriomas and deep infiltrating disease, excision is associated with lower recurrence rates than ablation. For superficial peritoneal endometriosis, systematic reviews have found no significant difference in pain outcomes between the two techniques.
  • Around 12% of women report no meaningful improvement in pain after surgical excision. Surgery helps the majority, but it is not a guarantee of relief.
  • Reoperation rates reach approximately 20% at two years and climb to 28–37% at five years. This figure is rarely part of the preoperative conversation, and it applies regardless of whether excision or ablation is performed.

Doctor's compass: bring these to your next appointment

→Given my disease stage and lesion type, what does the evidence actually show about excision versus ablation for my specific case?
→What is your personal reoperation rate for patients at my stage, and what factors most influence whether pain returns?
Burks et al. J Minim Invasive Gynecol · 2021 · Pickett et al. Reprod Fertil · 2023 · Abbott et al. Fertil Steril · 2004

Perimenopause Sleep FemSource

"What's the link between perimenopause and poor sleep?"

Progesterone decline changes the architecture of your sleep — not just how long you sleep, but how deeply

Bias & funding

Low risk

NIH-funded · No industry conflicts disclosed

Confidence

Moderate

Small objective studies · Consistent large-cohort self-report data

Peer review status

Peer reviewed · Published in accredited medical journals

Sponsors

NIH · No pharmaceutical sponsorship

Study population

Primary: n=20–189 (PSG) · Supporting cohort: n>3,000

Demography

Perimenopausal & postmenopausal women · Ages 40–60 · Predominantly North American & European cohorts

Citation count

Baker et al. 2015: 312 · Caufriez et al. 2011: 189 · Troìa et al. 2025: 14

Date published

2011 · 2015 · 2025

Why this matters to you

When progesterone drops, your brain spends less time in slow-wave sleep — the stage that consolidates memory, regulates cortisol, and leaves you feeling genuinely rested. You can sleep eight hours and still wake up exhausted because the hours themselves are no longer the problem. This is why behavioural fixes like sleep hygiene and earlier bedtimes often fail in perimenopause: they address the surface, not the cause. The research is clear that this is hormonal, structural, and measurable. Knowing that changes the conversation you need to have with your doctor.

What the research found

  • Waking exhausted after eight hours isn't anxiety or aging. Progesterone decline measurably shortens slow-wave sleep. The problem is architectural, not behavioural.
  • Progesterone replacement specifically targets disrupted sleep. It restores what's broken, but won't improve sleep that's already fine. Your vulnerability determines whether it helps.
  • Melatonin helps with sleep onset and circadian rhythm but doesn't address the hormonal root cause. For perimenopausal sleep disruption, it's unlikely to be sufficient on its own.

Doctor's compass: bring these to your next appointment

→Given what I'm experiencing, would checking my progesterone and estradiol levels help identify what's driving my sleep disruption?
→I've read that progesterone may specifically restore deep sleep when it's already disrupted. Is that worth considering for me, and what are the risks?
Baker et al. J Clin Endocrinol Metab · 2015 · Caufriez et al. J Clin Endocrinol Metab · 2011 · Troìa et al. J Clin Med · 2025
